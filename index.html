📚 Day 1 Vocabulary Panel

Before launching your tasks, review these key terms you will encounter in today's activities:

Households: Individuals or groups living together who own resources and buy finished goods. Real-Life Example: Your family, who supplies labor to employers and buys gas from a local station.
Businesses: Commercial firms that purchase resources in order to produce and sell finished goods. Real-Life Example: A tech company hiring software designers and manufacturing smartphones to sell.
Product Market: The conceptual place where businesses sell finished goods and services directly to consumers. Real-Life Example: You walking into a grocery store to buy a carton of milk or ordering shoes online.
Resource Market: The conceptual place where households sell their raw materials, capital, and labor to businesses. Real-Life Example: An adult applying for a job at a factory or a landowner renting retail space to a coffee shop.

📚 Day 2 Vocabulary Panel

Before launching your tasks, review these specific terms you will encounter in today's activities:

Expenditure: An amount of money spent on something; a payment or purchase. Real-Life Example: When you buy lunch or a business pays for a new machine, that spending is an expenditure.
Consumption: The act of buying and using up goods and services to satisfy personal wants. Real-Life Example: Buying a burrito or a movie ticket is consumer consumption—it's purchased for immediate personal use.
Inventory: A complete list or supply of goods, materials, or products currently kept in stock by a business. Real-Life Example: A shoe store keeping 200 pairs of sneakers in the back room that haven't been sold yet.
Investment: In macroeconomics, this refers strictly to business spending on physical assets to help them produce future goods, or additions to inventory. (It does NOT mean buying stocks or bonds!) Real-Life Example: A local bakery buying a brand-new commercial oven to bake more bread.
Exports: Goods and services that are produced inside one country and sold to buyers living in a completely different country. Real-Life Example: A United States dairy farm shipping Wisconsin cheese to a grocery store chain in France.
Imports: Goods and services that are produced in a foreign nation and bought by consumers over here. Real-Life Example: An American car dealership buying sedans manufactured at a factory located in Japan.
