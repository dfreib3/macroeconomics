Day 1: The Circular Flow Model

Welcome to Unit 2! To understand macroeconomics, we must track the entire system at once. The Circular Flow Model visualizes how different parts of an economy interact. Households provide land, labor, and capital in the Resource Market, while businesses turn those resources into finished products sold in the Product Market. Money moves continuously in the opposite direction.

📰 ECONOMY FLASH: Consumer Spending Rises as National Employment Climbs. Why? Income paid to workers in the resource market flows straight into business registers via the product market.

📚 Day 1 Vocabulary Panel
Macroeconomics: The study of the entire economy as a single system, tracking large-scale metrics like total output, inflation, and unemployment.
Focuses on national choices rather than individual choices.
Product Market: The arena where finished goods and services are bought by households and sold by businesses.
Examples include grocery stores, digital storefronts, and car dealerships.
Resource Market: The arena where businesses purchase the productive factors (labor, land, capital) provided by households.
Examples include corporate job interviews, office rental markets, and equipment leases.

Day 2: Components of GDP

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total dollar value of all final goods and services produced within a nation's borders in a specific year. Economists measure this output using the expenditure approach, summing up Consumer Spending (C), Business Investment (I), Government Spending (G), and Net Exports (NX).

📰 ECONOMY FLASH: Retail Inventory Expansion Signals Stronger Business Investment Forecasts. Remember: Unsold goods sitting on shelves are counted under Investment spending!

📚 Day 2 Vocabulary Panel
Expenditure Approach: The macroeconomic method that calculates GDP by adding together all spending on final goods and services.
Formula: GDP = C + I + G + NX
Investment Spending (I): Corporate spending on physical capital assets, machinery, new housing construction, and changes to factory inventories.
Does not refer to financial assets like stocks or bonds!
Net Exports (NX): The value of a nation's exports minus the value of its imports (Exports - Imports).
Accounts for foreign demand for domestic products while adjusting for local spending on foreign items.

Day 3: Limitations of GDP

Over the last two days, we learned how to calculate everything that a country produces using the expenditure formula ($GDP = C + I + G + NX$). It seems like a perfect way to score a country's success—the higher the number, the better the country, right? Not quite. Today, we look at the blind spots. GDP is a fantastic calculator for market transactions, but it is an incomplete tape measure for human well-being. A country could have a massive, skyrocketing GDP while its citizens suffer from severe pollution, extreme poverty, or exhausting work hours. Today, we discover exactly what GDP leaves out and why a high GDP doesn't always equal a high quality of life.

📰 ECONOMY FLASH: Factory Production Hits All-Time High; Local River Quality Drops to Historic Lows. Why does this matter? GDP counts the dollar value of every single widget the factory produces, but it completely ignores the toxic sludge dumped into the town's water supply.

📚 Day 3 Vocabulary Panel
Capita: Per person or per head (derived from Latin). In economics, dividing a country's total GDP by its population gives you "GDP per capita."
Real-Life Example: If a small island has a total GDP of $1,000,000 and a population of 100 people, its GDP per capita is $10,000 per person.
Nonmarket: Work, services, or production that are done privately at home rather than through an official commercial business.
Real-Life Example: Cleaning your own bedroom, cooking dinner for your family, or changing the oil in your own car. Because no market transaction occurs, GDP misses it entirely!
Informal: An economy's "underground" or unrecorded sector where transactions are kept off the books, often paid in cash or bartered to avoid taxes.
Real-Life Example: Paying a neighbor $20 in cash to help move a couch, or buying vegetables from an unregistered backyard garden stand.
Externality: A side effect or unintended consequence of an economic activity that affects bystanders who didn't choose to be involved.
Real-Life Example: A chemical plant making millions of dollars worth of plastics (adding to GDP) while releasing thick smog that causes respiratory issues for nearby families (which GDP ignores).
