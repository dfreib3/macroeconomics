Day 1: The Circular Flow Model

Learning Objective: Define macroeconomic performance metrics and visualize how resources, goods, and money move dynamically between households, businesses, and markets within the Circular Flow Model.

Key Vocabulary

Macroeconomics: The study of the entire economy as a single system, tracking large-scale metrics like total output, inflation, and unemployment.
Product Market: The arena where finished goods and services are bought by households and sold by businesses.
Resource Market: The arena where businesses purchase the productive factors (labor, land, capital) provided by households.

Day 2: Components of GDP

Learning Objective: Calculate national economic output using the expenditures approach formula (GDP = C + I + G + NX) and accurately classify transactions into their respective spending categories.

Key Vocabulary

Expenditure Approach: The macroeconomic method that calculates GDP by adding together all spending on final goods and services (GDP = C + I + G + NX).
Investment Spending (I): Corporate spending on physical capital assets, machinery, new housing construction, and changes to factory inventories. (Not stocks/bonds!)
Net Exports (NX): The value of a nation's exports minus the value of its imports (Exports - Imports).

Day 3: Limitations of GDP

Learning Objective: Evaluate the limitations of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) as a measure of economic well-being by identifying valuable economic activities and societal costs that standard GDP calculations fail to capture.

Key Vocabulary

Capita: Per person or per head. Dividing a country's total GDP by its population gives you "GDP per capita," showing the average output per person.
Nonmarket Activities: Work, services, or production done privately at home rather than through commercial businesses (e.g., cooking dinner or cleaning your own house) which GDP misses entirely.
Informal/Shadow Economy: Transactions kept completely off the books or paid under the table in cash to avoid tracking and taxes.
Externalities: Unintended side effects or societal costs—like pollution or health damages—that are ignored by standard GDP production tallies.
